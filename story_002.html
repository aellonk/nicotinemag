ARTIST FEATURE

Apolonia Sokol

Photography by

Emma Burlet

Writer, Anouk Judde, meets franco-polish painter, Apolonia Sokol, at her studio:

I enter her den where she introduces me to her new creations. I know her work from seeing it exhibited on several occasions. This time though, it’s different. I’m in front of her canvases and I feel like the characters are going to step out at any moment and come to life. I’m torn between a feeling of attraction to those mysterious figures and a sort of apprehension, the kind you could only feel towards an oracle.

Apolonia and I first met when she was part of One Long Changing Body, a group show at the Carlier Gebauer in Berlin and she was about to fly to Istanbul for a solo show at The Pill Gallery. When asked about her constant travel and she says “as a European, we have the possibility to go almost everywhere. Culture is inexhaustible, which makes it extremely exciting. We can always discover new things all the time.”

Generally, Apolonia paints about her encounters during her travels. Her surroundings, friends, loves, her brief affections. “Painters have always used their surroundings as models” the artist says. “You make love to your model or you paint someone you’ve loved, or admired. It’s simply natural.”

“Sometimes I paint without realizing I’m referencing another artist; Cro-Magnon, Egyptian, Roman, contemporary, Instagram ad... everything’s mixed. For the occidentals, its evident that the history of painting is rooted in our genetic code. The images we absorb everyday are all inspired by Art History, because Art is a language we’ve been using since the beginning of times.”

I, myself, have a hard time dissociating the work from the artist, even if I think that maybe it’s not really necessary, as they seem to be a single and shared identity. Painting for Apolonia is “vital, and I refuse to compromise it.”
